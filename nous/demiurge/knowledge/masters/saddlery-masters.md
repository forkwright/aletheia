# Historical Saddlery Masters

*Research compiled: 2026-01-30*
*Sources: Western Horseman, Texas State Historical Association, NEA, Autry Museum, Smithsonian*

---

## Hamley & Company (1883-present) — Pendleton, OR

**The Legend:**
- Founded by brothers Henry and J.J. Hamley in Ashton, South Dakota (1883)
- Moved to Pendleton, Oregon in 1905 — same building they operate from today
- **Oldest western store and saddle shop** — 140+ years continuous operation
- Craft traces back to William Hamley, passed through generations
- Survived economic downturns while maintaining quality standards

**Key Insight:** Longevity through consistency, not novelty.

---

## Visalia Stock Saddle Company (1860s-1970s) — California

**The Innovation:**
- Founded ~1860s in Visalia, CA; David E. Walker bought company 1870
- **Key craftsmen:** Ricardo Mattley (carved oak trees), Juan Martarell (leatherwork), Alsalio Herrera (silversmithing)
- **Revolutionary design:** Lacked tall horns and long stirrups of Spanish saddles; protective skirts for riders' legs
- Saddle trees cut from **native oak crotches** and hand-carved
- Moved to San Francisco 1887

**Key Insight:** Regional adaptation (Mexican vaquero heritage) + material mastery (oak crotch selection) = distinctive style.

## S.D. Myres Saddle Company — Texas

**Samuel Dale "Tio Sam" Myres (1871-1953):**
- **Training:** 3-year apprenticeship with T.R. James and Sons Saddlers, Cleburne, Texas
- Started at age 15
- **Famous for:** "Miller $10,000 Saddle" (1914) — 15 lbs silver/gold, 300+ precious stones
- **Signature style:** Oak-leaf designs, Charles Russell artwork patterns
- **Production range:** $40 working saddles to elaborate Hollywood pieces
- **Famous clients:** Gene Autry, John Wayne, Tom Mix
- **War contribution:** McClellan saddles during WWI, government citation
- Started Sweetwater (1898), moved El Paso (1920), operated until 1969

**Key Insight:** Range matters — working saddles AND art pieces. Training started young, lasted years.

## Al Furstnow — Montana

**The Innovator:**
- Partnership with Charlie Coggshall in Miles City, Montana
- Most significant operation between Billings and Dickinson
- **Perfected the Montana Saddle Tree** — became industry standard
- Developed **swell fork** and **flat-plate rigging** systems

**Revolutionary Method:**
> "Revolutionary blend of assembly line techniques from back East with custom care of traditional one-man western shops"

- Employed dozens of workers specializing in specific aspects
- Made "thousands and thousands of saddles"
- Partnership split 1899 — competition drove further innovation

**Key Insight:** Specialization + assembly doesn't mean loss of quality. This is the component inventory concept.

## Edward H. Bohlin — Hollywood Era

**The Transition to Art:**
- Met Tom Mix while performing at Pantages Theatre
- "Saddlemaker and Silversmith to the Stars"
- Created **12,000+ saddles** including Tournament of Roses Parade pieces
- **Luxury technique:** Many handcrafted silver pieces per saddle
- Clients: Hollywood stars, politicians, kings, emperors, Texas oil barons
- **Motto:** "It's the High Class Merchandise that Really Counts"

**Key Insight:** Craft can become art when function is already mastered. Don't skip the function.

## Don King — Wyoming

**NEA Heritage Award Winner:**
- Developed **"Sheridan-style" saddle**
- **Signature technique:** Wild roses viewed from 45-degree angle
- Complex scroll-like patterns of interlocking circles
- **Impeccable craftsmanship and incredible precision**
- Meticulous balanced design: flowers, leaves, stems in circular, orderly patterns
- Don King Museum in Sheridan showcases collection
- Mentored craftsmen like Keith Valley

**Key Insight:** Precision is a style. A signature technique becomes identity.

## Master vs. Journeyman: The Distinction

| Attribute | Master | Journeyman |
|-----------|--------|------------|
| **Materials** | Premium leather, carefully selected oak crotches | Standard materials |
| **Precision** | Incredible precision in every detail | Acceptable uniformity |
| **Innovation** | Developed new techniques | Followed established patterns |
| **Artistic vision** | Integrated art beyond functionality | Functional focus |
| **Durability** | Outlasted the cowboys who rode them | Seasonal use |
| **Custom work** | Measured horse and rider individually | Standard sizing |
| **Legacy** | Took and trained apprentices | Produced work |

## Lost Tools and Techniques

- **Hand-carved oak crotch saddle trees** — understanding which crotches produce strongest, most flexible trees
- **Native oak selection** — the "eye" for material
- **Traditional rigging methods** (flat-plate systems)
- **Hand-forged hardware**
- **Natural dyes and traditional leather preparation**
- **Sgraffito technique** — nearly vanished Eastern European leather carving
- **Hand-stitched construction throughout**
- **Traditional pattern-making** from individual measurements
- **Master's eye** for material selection and quality assessment

## Training and Apprenticeship

### Historical Path
1. **Apprenticeship:** 3-7 years starting as teenagers (Myres started at 15)
2. **Journeyman period:** Travel between master shops to learn different techniques
3. **Masterpiece requirement:** Create demonstration piece judged by established masters
4. **Specialization:** Gradual focus on trees, leatherwork, silversmithing, or carving
5. **Regional styles:** Learn local traditions (California vaquero, Texas working, Montana innovations)

### Modern Reality
> "Traditional apprenticeships all but gone now" — contemporary makers

**Exception:** Sheridan School of Saddlery maintains traditional apprenticeship model

## Core Insight

> Master saddlery was distinguished by the intersection of **functional excellence, artistic vision, technical innovation, and generational knowledge transfer** — qualities increasingly rare in modern mass production.

The masters didn't choose between quality and efficiency. They found ways to achieve both — through specialization, component systems, and relentless attention to the details that matter.
