# Ardent Belt Development Summary
*Key Changes & Updates from Product Development Sessions*

## Critical Updates Made During Development

### 1. HARDWARE UPGRADE (Major Change)
**FROM**: Buckle Guy solid brass heel bar (#10174)
- Cost: $4.34 each
- Source: Modern casting facility

**TO**: Abbey England Flat Whole Harness Buckle (B075ALX)  
- Cost: £11.70 each (~$18 landed)
- Source: 1832 Walsall foundry, Royal Warrant
- **Impact**: +$13.66 per belt material cost
- **Rationale**: Heritage alignment, sand-cast quality, heirloom durability

### 2. ATTACHMENT METHOD (Major Design Change)
**FROM**: Chicago screws (removable hardware)
- Allows buckle swapping
- Modern convenience approach

**TO**: Stitched attachment (permanent)
- Buckle and belt age together as one piece
- Traditional saddlery method
- **Philosophy**: "The buckle belongs to this belt. They age together."

### 3. THREAD SPECIFICATION CORRECTIONS
**EARLY ERROR**: Listed as "Ritza Tiger" in some documents
**CORRECTED TO**: Fil au Chinois Lin Câblé
- Size: 332 (thickest) not 532
- Color: Sans (natural/ecru)
- **Note**: Sizing is inverse - lower number = thicker thread

### 4. PRICING EVOLUTION
- **Initial**: $100
- **Interim**: $110 (shipping included)  
- **Final**: $150 
- **Driver**: Heritage buckle cost increase + true material costing

### 5. EDGE TREATMENT UPGRADE
**ADDED**: Hot edge creasing with Made on Jupiter M3000
- Tool investment: ~$480 for complete kit
- **Process**: Hot crease → sand → burnish → Ardent wax
- **Rationale**: Traditional harness finishing, functional durability

## Production Process Refinements

### Skiving Specifications Updated:
- **Depth**: 67% reduction (traditional method)
- **Location**: Flesh side at buckle fold
- **Purpose**: Reduces bulk at fold while maintaining strength

### Keeper Construction Detailed:
- **Material**: Wickett & Craig harness leather
- **Dimensions**: 1/2"-3/4" width × 4.75-5" length  
- **Construction**: Floating between stitched sections
- **Edge Treatment**: Full finishing (sand, burnish, wax)

### Quality Control Additions:
- **Date Stamping**: YYYYMMDD format inside by keeper
- **Hole Finishing**: Rod burnishing for smooth edges
- **Thread Flattening**: Vergez Blanchard saddler hammer

## Philosophy Alignment Strengthened

### Core Principle Clarified:
**"Heritage over modern when it produces better durability"**

### Material Selection Rationale:
- Hermann Oak: 140-year American tannery tradition
- Abbey England: 1832 Walsall foundry with Royal Warrant  
- Fil au Chinois: French mill since 1898, master thread makers
- No synthetics except when absolutely necessary

### Design Decision Framework:
1. **Functional first** - does it improve the belt's purpose?
2. **Heritage methods** - traditional when superior to modern
3. **Provenance matters** - know who makes what you use
4. **Heirloom quality** - built to last generations, not seasons

## Lessons Learned

### Key Insights:
1. **True costing matters** - initial estimates were too low
2. **Heritage suppliers exist** - worth finding and paying for quality
3. **Traditional methods have reasons** - hot creasing isn't decoration
4. **Philosophy drives decisions** - every choice reflects brand values
5. **Tools are investments** - quality tools enable quality work

### Process Improvements:
- Research suppliers thoroughly before committing
- Account for import duties and shipping in cost calculations  
- Test processes on scrap before finalizing procedures
- Document everything - small details matter in production
